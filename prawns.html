Prawns

Overview


Prawn allergies, like other seafood allergies, occur due to an immune system reaction to specific proteins found in prawns. The main allergens in prawns are tropomyosin, arginine kinase, and myosin light chain, among others. When these proteins enter the body, the immune system can mistake them for harmful substances, triggering an allergic response.


The exact cause of why some individuals develop prawn allergies while others don’t isn’t fully understood, but several factors contribute to the development of this condition. Genetics plays a role, as allergies often run in families. Also, a history of other allergies or asthma may increase the likelihood of developing a prawn allergy.

When someone with a prawn allergy consumes prawns or even comes into contact with prawn proteins (such as through cooking vapors or touching raw prawns), their immune system reacts by producing antibodies called immunoglobulin E (IgE). These antibodies recognize the prawn proteins as harmful and release chemicals, such as histamine, into the bloodstream, causing allergic symptoms.


Symptoms of a prawn allergy can range from mild to severe and may include hives, itching, swelling (especially of the lips, face, throat, or tongue), digestive issues, difficulty breathing, or anaphylaxis, which is a potentially life-threatening reaction requiring immediate medical attention.


Avoiding prawns and products containing prawns or prawn derivatives is the primary way to prevent allergic reactions. Reading food labels carefully, informing restaurants about the allergy, and being cautious about cross-contamination during food preparation are essential for those with prawn allergies.


In Conclusion

In case of accidental exposure or an allergic reaction, antihistamines or epinephrine (for severe reactions) may be used as emergency treatments. Allergy testing by an allergist can confirm a prawn allergy and provide guidance on managing and avoiding potential allergens.
