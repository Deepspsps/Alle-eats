Fish

Overview


Fish, like prawns and other seafood, contains proteins that can trigger allergic reactions in susceptible individuals. The proteins in fish that most commonly cause allergic responses are parvalbumins. When someone with a fish allergy consumes fish or inhales cooking vapors from fish, their immune system can react abnormally to these proteins.


When a person with a fish allergy ingests fish or comes into contact with fish proteins, their immune system perceives these proteins as harmful invaders and produces immunoglobulin E (IgE) antibodies in response. The IgE antibodies then trigger the release of chemicals, such as histamine, into the bloodstream. These chemicals are responsible for causing the symptoms of an allergic reaction.


Fish allergy symptoms can vary in severity and may include skin reactions (such as hives, itching, or eczema), gastrointestinal issues (such as nausea, vomiting, or diarrhea), respiratory problems (such as wheezing or difficulty breathing), swelling of the face, lips, tongue, or throat, and in severe cases, anaphylaxis, a potentially life-threatening reaction requiring immediate medical attention.


Like with other food allergies, avoiding fish and fish products is crucial for those with fish allergies. Reading food labels attentively, informing restaurants about the allergy, and being cautious about cross-contact during food preparation are essential practices. Additionally, individuals with fish allergies should also be wary of certain supplements, medications, and even skin care products that might contain fish-derived ingredients.


In Conclusion

Allergies to specific types of fish can vary among individuals, and some may be allergic to one type of fish while tolerating others. An allergist can perform tests to identify specific fish allergies and provide guidance on managing and avoiding potential allergens.
