Kiwi

Overview


Kiwi allergy occurs when the immune system mistakenly identifies proteins in kiwi fruit as harmful and triggers an allergic reaction. The main proteins responsible for kiwi allergies are actinidin and various other proteins found in the fruit.


When someone with a kiwi allergy consumes the fruit, their immune system may produce specific antibodies called immunoglobulin E (IgE). Upon subsequent exposure to kiwi proteins, these IgE antibodies signal immune cells to release histamine and other chemicals, leading to allergic symptoms.



Kiwi allergy symptoms can range from mild to severe and may include:

1. Skin Reactions: Hives, redness, or eczema may develop on the skin after contact with kiwi.

2. Digestive Issues: Some individuals may experience nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, or diarrhea.

3. Respiratory Problems:Wheezing, coughing, nasal congestion, or difficulty breathing can occur, particularly in more severe cases.

4. Oral Allergy Syndrome (OAS): Many people with kiwi allergies experience OAS, which causes itching or swelling of the lips, tongue, and throat. OAS is often a milder form of allergic reaction and is linked to cross-reactivity with pollen allergens.


Severe allergic reactions to kiwi, such as anaphylaxis, are rare but can be life-threatening. Anaphylaxis involves a sudden drop in blood pressure, difficulty breathing, and systemic shock.

Cross-reactivity is a notable factor in kiwi allergies, especially for individuals allergic to latex. The proteins in kiwi are structurally similar to those in latex, leading to a phenomenon known as latex-fruit syndrome. In such cases, individuals allergic to latex may also experience allergic reactions to kiwi and other fruits like bananas, avocados, and chestnuts.


In Conclusion

Avoidance of kiwi and kiwi-containing products is the primary strategy for managing kiwi allergies. Reading food labels carefully and informing healthcare providers about the allergy is crucial for avoiding accidental exposure. Allergic individuals may need to carry an epinephrine auto-injector for emergency treatment in case of severe reactions. Consulting with an allergist is essential for proper diagnosis, management, and guidance on living with a kiwi allergy.
